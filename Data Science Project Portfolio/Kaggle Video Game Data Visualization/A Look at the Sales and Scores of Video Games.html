Answering Question 1

How has each platform performed in terms of titles released and global sales?

Video game consoles and platforms tend to follow “generations” of release, which coincide with the releases of new, next-generation consoles. For instance: The first generation of video games saw the introduction of Pong and many copy-cats. Consoles released in concurrent generations tend to compete most heavily with each other.

Nintendo would introduce their first console in the first generation, a dedicated system called the Color TV-Game, and would subsequently introduce new systems each generation from the 3rd generation onwards with the NES, SNES, N64, GCN, Wii and Wii U.

Early on, Nintendo would face competition from SEGA, lasting from the 3rd generation up to the 6th, where SEGA would withdraw from the video game console market after the poor performance of the Dreamcast.

Sony would make its first, highly successful foray into the video game hardware market in the 5th generation, with its highly-popular PlayStation. Sony would then go on to release the PS2, PS3 and PS4 in subsequent generations.

Microsoft made its way onto the video game hardware stage in the 6th generation with the introduction of the Xbox, shaking up the Japanese-dominated market. Afterwards, it would go on to release the Xbox 360 and Xbox One in subsequent generations.

We will compare number of title releases and video game software unit sales over time for home consoles that were released in concurrent generations, as follows below:

4th Generation: Nintendo SNES, SEGA Genesis

5th Generation: Nintendo 64, SEGA Saturn, Sony PlayStation

6th Generation: Microsoft Xbox, Nintendo GameCube, SEGA Dreamcast, Sony PlayStation 2

7th Generation: Microsoft Xbox 360, Nintendo Wii, Sony PlayStation 3

8th Generation: Microsoft Xbox ONE, Nintendo Wii U, Sony PlayStation 4

Nintendo’s SNES would dominate the 4th generation, easily surpassing the SEGA Genesis in terms of library size and video game software units sold.

Sony’s PlayStation enjoyed massive popularity and an expansive library in the 5th generation.

Sony’s PlayStation 2 is the best-selling video game console of all time, and thus would enjoy a vast library of games and very high software sales.

The Wii was highly popular in the early years after its release. The Xbox 360 and PS3 would have more staying power however, and they essentially traded blows when it came to new title releases and software unit sales throughout the 7th generation.

Sony would again see massive popularity with its PS4, consistently being ahead of the Xbox One and far ahead of the Wii U in terms of titles released and video game software unit sales.

Answering Question 2

Do certain regions prefer certain genres?

The genre of a video game describes what conventions its gameplay follows. Some examples of genres are:

Action: Gameplay revolves around overcoming challenges, and emphasizes hand-eye coordination and reaction time. Subgenres may include shooters, platformers and fighting games.

Adventure: Gameplay places focus on narrative story-telling, puzzle-solving and exploration.

Role-playing: Gameplay involves controlling a character in a well-established world. Emphasis is placed on decision-making, story-telling, and player character development.

Simulation: Gameplay can be highly varied, but in general is meant to simulate real-world activities to a high degree of accuracy.

Sports: A subgenre of simulation. Gameplay simulates the practice of physical sports such as football.

Racing: Gameplay involves partaking in a racing competition, using any manner of land, sea, air or space vehicle.

Puzzle: Gameplay can be highly varied, but in general emphasizes puzzle-solving capabilities or logical reasoning.

In order to answer the question of which regions prefer which genres, we will break down the total regional video game software unit sales by genre.

North America, Europe, and the other regions follow the same pattern for the 1st, 2nd and 3rd most popular genres, being action, sports and shooters respectively. The next most popular genres tend to be platformers, racing, fighting and role-playing.

However, in Japan, role-playing games are massively popular, and shooters are the least popular genre. In terms of total sales in Japan, role-playing games have sold more than twice as many units as action, the next most popular genre in Japan.

Answering Question 3

How well do games sell, based on their ESRB rating?

The Entertainment Software Rating Board (ESRB) is the North American rating organization responsible for assigning ratings to video games, based on the age-appropriateness of their content. Similar rating boards are based in Japan (CERO) and Europe (PEGI), which rate video games in their respective regions.

The ratings that the ESRB uses include:

Early Childhood (eC): Suitable for a preschool audience.

Everyone (E): Generally suitable for everyone. Prior to 1998, this rating was called Kids to Adults (K-A).

Everyone 10+ (E10+): Contains content that is unsuitable for children under 10 years old. Introduced in 2005.

Teen (T): Contains content that is suitable only for teenagers aged 13 and above.

Mature (M): Contains content that is suitable only for those aged 17 and above.

Adults Only (AO): Contains content that is suitable only for those aged 18 and above. This rating is used for when the impact of the content exceeds that covered by the M rating.

Rating Pending (RP): A placeholder rating used in promotional materials for a game prior to its release, before it has been assigned an official rating by the ESRB.

A game’s rating may affect its sales in a number of ways. A game rated E is accessible to more potential gamers, but a game rated M may attract more gamers due to being “cooler.”

In order to answer the question, we will look at total sales of games by rating, average sales of games by rating, and global sales over the years, broken down by rating.

We can see that games rated E account for the highest number of total global sales, though this is a product of the fact that most games being released are rated E. Disregarding AO as that rating contains incredibly niche and sometimes frankly disturbing entries, and knowing that K-A is simply an earlier version of E, we see that games rated M tend to sell more on average than lower-rated titles.

Between 2000 and 2010, the proportion of “child-friendly” games (E10+ and below) was over half of all sales, which suggests that the accessibility of a game was more important to its sales in this time period. However, from 2008 onwards we see that the absolute number of games that are rated M remains relatively constant, compared to the shrinking number of lower-rated games being sold. This may be due to the more casual gamers who might be drawn to lower-rated games shifting over to mobile gaming, while the more “hardcore” gamers who would play higher-rated games stuck with dedicated gaming hardware.

Answering Question 4

Which games do critic opinions and user opinions differ on most?

As with all other forms of entertainment media, there is room for disagreement between professional critics and casual players on the quality of a game.

The reasons for why the opinions of professional reviewers and the opinions of casual reviewers may differ are many, so we won’t go into too much detail on them.

We are interested however in which games this difference of opinion occurs the greatest in. The review aggregation site Metacritic provides the average scores of several video games, with the average scores of professional critics being given as a Metascore. Site users are also able to submit reviews, which are then aggregated as an average User Score.

In order to answer this question, we will limit ourselves to games with over 10 critic reviews and 50 user reviews. Otherwise, we will get results including games that are satirically declared by users to be “masterpieces.” We will calculate the difference between User Score values, and Critic Score values (Taking the average if a game appears on more than one platform), and find which games have the highest difference (loved by users, panned by critics), and which have the lowest (panned by users, loved by critics).

Answering Question 5

Who are the top performing publishers, and what is their distribution of published game genres?

Different publishers will have different preferences for games that they publish. EA notably sees very high sales when it comes to its shooters and sports games, and have entire studios dedicated to their development (DICE and EA Sports respectively). Nintendo is well-known for its best-selling platformer series Super Mario, best-selling role-playing series Pokémon, and best-selling action-adventure series The Legend of Zelda. Activision consistently sees high sales when it comes to its Call of Duty franchise.

Takeaways

The action genre is the most popular genre in terms of sales and titles released. This is likely due to the relative ease that one can settle down, learn the ropes of the game and play. Compared to genres like role-playing and simulation games which sometimes demand a significant time investment, and fighting and strategy games which sometimes have a steep learning curve, action games and their subgenres lend themselves more to pick-up-and-go gameplay. For this reason, we see that the top video game publishers tend to publish more action games.

Critic opinion and user opinion can differ on video games for many reasons. The greatest driving force that causes this dissonance between professional and casual reviewers is usually emotional. A game that causes significant controversy or outrage can lead to “review bombing,” where users flood the offending game in question with the worst possible reviews. Conversely, users with a positive emotional attachment to a specific franchise may enjoy a particular game within that franchise more, compared to a reviewer who judges it in a vacuum.

Japan really loves their role-playing games.
